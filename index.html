  • Fall 2020
    EECS 376: Foundations of Computer Science

    This was one of my favorite courses I’ve taken so far. Unlike most of my other coding-intensive EECS classes, this felt more like a math class characterized by formal proofs and requiring a different approach to problem solving. Some of the most important and interesting concepts covered were analyzing algorithms, Turing machines, the classes of P and NP, and randomness and cryptography. I thoroughly enjoyed learning about these new concepts and found that this class influenced my approach to critically thinking about abstract concepts. I also loved typesetting my assignments in LaTeX :)

    EECS 370 : Computer Organization

    In all of my previous EECS classes, I had learned how to express tasks in terms of high-level programming languages like C++. In this class, I was introduced to a whole different world and learned more about how a low-level language is executed by the hardware. The majority of the class involved building projects like simulators and linkers for a fictional assembly language called LC-2K, but I was also given an introduction to important concepts like caching and virtual memory. Ultimately, this class served as an effective introduction to low-level computing.

    SI 311: Special Topics in Information

    This was a special topics course in Sports Analytics that I took with the Dean of the School of Information. It involved a “classroom component” of weekly lectures in which we learned about prevailing theories in sports analytics as well as a “field component” in which we were assigned to work with a team within the university’s athletic department. I worked with the Women’s Lacrosse team, assisting with their adoption of a fitness-tracking technology and creating tools that were subsequently integrated into their daily protocol. This was a fantastic opportunity for me to get experience working with a smaller, yet disciplined, organization of non-technical professionals that allowed me to take control of my own project.

    ENGR 100: Introduction to Engineering

    This was an introductory course required for all students in the College of Engineering. I enrolled in the Food Science section with the purpose of learning more about a discipline that I was unfamiliar with. The course culminated in a final project through which our group recommended a new equity design and packaging prototype to our client, General Mills. The experience of consulting with a major corporation as well as bolstering my technical communication skills proved to be valuable.

  • Winter 2020
    EECS 281: Data Structures and Algorithms

    This course may be notorious for its time-consuming projects and difficult exams, but I believe it should really be notable for its interesting and extremely practical content. I can say with certainty that this is my favorite class that I’ve taken to date. I learned about various data structures like maps, sets, graphs, stacks, and queues, as well as algorithm families like dynamic programming, branch and bound, backtracking, and greedy. I was challenged to apply myself through the very involved projects and ultimately, my ability to think critically about the design of software was significantly bolstered.

    EECS 201: Computer Science Pragmatics

    I strongly believe that anybody interested in software development should take this course. I learned so many tips and tricks about text editors, operating systems, and command line interfaces that I thought were inconsequential at the time, but have proven to be immensely useful when working on side projects and during internships.

    PSYCH 111: Introduction to Psychology

    This was the only non-technical course I took during the semester, and it also ended up being one of the most interesting courses I have taken to date. I’ve always been fascinated with the human brain and what drives decision making, and this class helped satiate my curiosity. It also introduced me to a subject that regardless of my degree requirements, I am very interested in learning more about.

    IOE 265: Probability and Statistics

    I registered for this course out of mere chance: the other classes that fulfill my degree’s STATS requirements were already at capacity. Despite this stressful scheduling start and the unforeseen impact COVID-19 had on this semester, I learned a lot about the fields of probability and statistics and got full value out of this class. I recommend this course to anyone who needs to fulfill a requirement.

  • Fall 2019
    EECS 203: Discrete Mathematics

    As someone who was always interested in math but most recently familiar with calculus, this class was a great introduction to “a different side” of mathematics. The most important thing I gained from this class was a foundation for proof-based mathematics through the formal introduction of concepts like propositional logic, induction, sets, counting, discrete probability, and graph theory. The emphasis on logical problem solving instilled in me by this class also pervaded different aspects of my education as well as my life, and ultimately inspired me to pursue a Mathematics minor.

    EECS 280: Programming and Introductory Data Structures

    My first semester of college marked the beginning of a rule that allowed freshmen to take this class in their first semester. Seeing that I fulfilled the requirements and had previous experience coding in C++, I snatched up the opportunity and didn’t look back. I thoroughly enjoyed learning about basic data structures and algorithms and applying myself through unique projects. Ultimately, being able to experience the structure of coding classes at Michigan so early on set a stable foundation for all my future classes.

    ECON 101: Principles of Economics

    This is a class that I believe everyone should take. Regardless of major, learning the basic economic principles that guide the society we live in is extremely important. This class taught me the importance of learning about subjects unrelated to my major.
